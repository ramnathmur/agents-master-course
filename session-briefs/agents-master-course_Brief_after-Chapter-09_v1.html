Chapter 00 · The Toolsmith · Trait: None. Plumbing only.

Setup: Claude Max + SDK + IntelliJ

I worked through Setup: Claude Max + SDK + IntelliJ. The chapter focused on none. plumbing only.. What I'm carrying forward: The SDK shells to the CLI. Configuration precedence is silent. Verify your env, not your assumption.

  • **The SDK is a CLI wrapper.** `claude — agent-sdk` is not a fresh HTTP client. It shells to the `claude` CLI binary that `pip install` brought along. That CLI owns the OAuth session. Your Python code never sees an API key on the Max path.
  • **Auth precedence is silent, not loud.** If `ANTHROPIC_API_KEY` is set, the SDK uses it without a warning. There is no "you have two auth sources, which one?" prompt. The architect's discipline — explicit `unset` or empty value in every run config you create.
Carry-forward: The SDK shells to the CLI. Configuration precedence is silent. Verify your env, not your assumption.

Chapter 06 · The Planner · Trait: Reasoning isolated. Schema as grammar.

Reason: Deliberate Planning + Tool Design

"Schema is grammar. Loose grammar, loose thought. Required fields are how you compress reasoning quality into the contract."

I worked through Reason: Deliberate Planning + Tool Design. The chapter focused on reasoning isolated. schema as grammar.. What I'm carrying forward: Schema is the agent's grammar. Required fields compress reasoning quality into the contract. Every field has a teaching reason, not just a data reason.

  • Required fields are required — no `Optional[T] = None` fields without a server-side default.
  • Enums over strings — for any field with a finite value set, use an enum. 100x reliability gain.
  • Field-level descriptions — every field's `description` answers "HOW to fill this," not just "WHAT shape."
  • Server-side validation — the tool body re-checks the schema. The model WILL produce malformed inputs occasionally; the tool's rejection becomes a teaching signal.
Carry-forward: Schema is the agent's grammar. Required fields compress reasoning quality into the contract. Every field has a teaching reason, not just a data reason.
Chapter 07 · The Operator · Trait: Action isolated. Inline `permission_mode` + `PreToolUse` / `PostToolUse` hooks.

Act Under Permission: Hooks and Guardrails

I worked through Act Under Permission: Hooks and Guardrails. The chapter focused on action isolated. inline `permission_mode` + `pretooluse` / `posttooluse` hooks.. What I'm carrying forward: Hooks are the architect's leverage point. Cross-cutting policy lives in hooks; tool-specific correctness lives in tools. The deny reason is not a refusal — it's a teaching signal to the model.

  • **Map the hook taxonomy onto your code.** Three roles, all now live in `chapter_07_act.py` — - **Guard** — `sandbox_guard` (PreToolUse). Prevent bad actions before they happen. `permission_mode="default"` is the coarse layer; the Guard hook is the surgical one.
  • **Treat `permission_mode` as posture, hooks as policy.** `permission_mode` is your overall trust level. Hooks are your specific rules. In production, you want low trust + specific allow — lists. In dev, you want high trust + watchful Observers (like `audit_observer`). Don't confuse the two layers.
  • **Treat deny as a teaching signal.** A denied tool call is not a failure — it's structured information the model can reason about. Write your deny reasons (in `sandbox_guard`) like you'd write a help-desk ticket response: explain what was wrong, suggest what to try instead. The model will use it.
Carry-forward: Hooks are the architect's leverage point. Cross-cutting policy lives in hooks; tool-specific correctness lives in tools. The deny reason is not a refusal — it's a teaching signal to the model.
Chapter 08 · The Architect · Trait: All five composed. Eval harness.

Five Traits Together + Eval Harness

"Five traits in one runnable file, scored by an eval set you wrote yourself. Spend a third of your build time here — it pays back over the next four chapters."

I worked through Five Traits Together + Eval Harness. The chapter focused on all five composed. eval harness.. What I'm carrying forward: Evals are the unlock for iteration. 30% of build time. Every fix becomes a regression test. Without this, you're vibing — not engineering.

  • Outcome vs trajectory — am I scoring the final state, the path taken, or both? Use outcome for fast feedback, trajectory for promotion gates.
  • Deterministic vs LLM-judge — Python comparison where possible; LLM-judge only for genuinely fuzzy outputs.
  • Eval-set hygiene — is my eval set checked into the repo? Does it grow on every shipped bugfix? Do I keep a held-out set for periodic checks?
  • Regression discipline — did I re-run ALL cases after this change, not just the formerly-failing one?
  • Failure attribution — when a case fails, can I tell whether it's the prompt, the tool, or the goal predicate that's wrong?
  • Tracing as architecture — does every tool call emit a span with (a) a unique **span ID**, (b) a **parent reference** so the call tree reconstructs, (c) a **structured event shape** (dataclass or schema — not a free-form log string), and (d) an **append-only JSONL artifact** on disk? These four properties are the contract. OpenTelemetry is the industry standard for this shape — learn that the standard exists, then know that your `traces.jsonl` in Chapter 8 is a deliberately minimal subset of it. A trace you can't replay is a trace you don't have.
Carry-forward: Evals are the unlock for iteration. 30% of build time. Every fix becomes a regression test. Without this, you're vibing — not engineering.
Chapter 09 · The Reviewer · Trait: All five + actor/judge separation.

Deeper Integration: Verifier / Critic Loop

"Don't ask the model 'is your answer correct?' — that's confirmation, not verification. Give me a structurally different judge."

I worked through Deeper Integration: Verifier / Critic Loop. The chapter focused on all five + actor/judge separation.. What I'm carrying forward: Actor and judge must be structurally different. Verify the critic with known-bad inputs before trusting its accept signals.

  • Adversarial framing — does the critic's system prompt prime skepticism? ("Find what's wrong" not "Is this good?")
  • Calibration set — do I have 5 known-good + 5 known-bad outputs that I score the critic against? Did the critic accept all 5 good and reject all 5 bad?
  • Separation discipline — is the critic in a fresh context, with a different system prompt, ideally a different model tier?
  • Cost gate — am I running the critic on every output (2x cost), or only on high-stakes ones?
  • Verification ≠ re-planning — does my critic say "this is wrong" without saying "here's how to fix it"? Fixing is Chapter 10's surface.
Carry-forward: Actor and judge must be structurally different. Verify the critic with known-bad inputs before trusting its accept signals.
